DRY

The built-in classes, modules and methods in Ruby serve as a great example on this topic. What do hashes, arrays and ranges have in common? They are all collection objects that can be iterated over and thus some of their methods will be similar. So Ruby in all of its magnificent efficiency kills three birds with one stone. This ultimately reduces duplication and avoids confusion.

SINGLE INHERITANCE & OTHER NICE THINGS

Another point of note about modules is that they are a nifty way around the single inheritance rule for classes in Ruby. They also allow you to group like behaviours that may not be associated with a particular class. Or you can spread them across several different classes without having to establish a parent/child relationship.

HOW DO YOU CHOOSE

So where do modules come in when writing your own classes and methods? In the Well Grounded Rubyist, David Black gives a couple of handy guidelines to help you make good choices. The most important one we already talked about, grouping like behaviors that may fit in in several different classes--and many times modules are named to indicate this, they are adjectives and objects are nouns by convention. Choosing modules and working with them effectively is a big topic, but hopefully this little intro will help jumpstart my understanding!
